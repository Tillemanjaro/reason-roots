Three questions beneath the surface

The unity of the Catholic Church is not uniformity. It is communion â€” a shared sacramental life, creed, and relationship with the Bishop of Rome, expressed in radically different cultural forms. The 24 liturgical rites in full communion with Rome are evidence that unity and diversity are not opposites in Catholic ecclesiology. The hard question is whether diversity is allowed, and how much diversity the structures of communion can hold â€” a question the Church debates actively and imperfectly.

Missionary history includes real failures: complicity with colonial power, destruction of indigenous cultures, forced conversions, and the residential school systems in North America and Australia. The Church has formally acknowledged these failures. Pope Francis asked forgiveness from indigenous peoples in Canada in 2022 in one of the most direct papal apologies in modern history. Acknowledging this history does not erase it â€” and it does not erase the genuine good that was also done. Both are true.

Both, depending on where you look. Catholicism is declining in Western Europe and parts of North America. It is growing rapidly in Sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Asia, and holding steady in Latin America despite significant losses to Pentecostalism. The global Catholic population has grown from roughly 600 million in 1970 to 1.4 billion today. The center of gravity has shifted south and east. The Church's future looks less like Rome and more like Lagos, Manila, and Lima.
