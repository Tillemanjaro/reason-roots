Faith & Science

The conflict narrative - that religion and science have been at war since Galileo - is one of the most historically inaccurate ideas in popular culture. The Church that built the first universities, funded the Gregorian calendar, and produced the priest who proposed the Big Bang is not the enemy of scientific inquiry. This chapter examines what actually happened.

The most widespread cultural narrative about religion and science is the "conflict narrative" - that they have been at war since Galileo, and that faith is the enemy of reason. Historians of science - not theologians - have spent the last three decades dismantling it.

Faith and reason are like two wings on which the human spirit rises to the contemplation of truth. - Pope John Paul II, Fides et Ratio (1998)

The Catholic intellectual tradition does not require curiosity to shrink. For two thousand years it has asked whether the order studied by science and the meaning sought by philosophy might belong to one reality - and answered yes.

Catholic Scientists

The priests and monks who changed science

The history of science includes an extraordinary number of Catholic clergy and faithful whose faith motivated rather than hindered their inquiry. They studied nature because they believed nature was intelligible and that its intelligibility was a gift.

Who he was

An Augustinian friar and abbot whose monastery garden in Brno became one of the most consequential scientific sites in history.

What he discovered

Through meticulous pea breeding experiments, Mendel identified the basic laws of inheritance - dominant and recessive traits, ratios of expression.

His legacy

His work, ignored for decades, was rediscovered in 1900 and became the foundation of modern genetics and evolutionary biology.

Genetics was founded in a monastery.

The Real History

What actually happened

The "conflict thesis" - the idea that science and religion have been perpetually at war - was popularized in the 19th century by two polemicists: John William Draper (1874) and Andrew Dickson White (1896). Historians of science have largely rejected it. The actual history is considerably more interesting.

Galileo

The most famous case, and what actually happened

Galileo Galilei is the centerpiece of the conflict narrative. What actually happened is considerably more complicated than the popular story.

The popular story
The Church silenced Galileo for saying the Earth moves. Science versus religion. The actual history
Galileo's condemnation in 1633 was driven more by his mockery of Pope Urban VIII - a former patron - than by heliocentrism itself. His faith
Galileo remained Catholic his entire life. He never left the Church and saw no contradiction between his science and his faith. His punishment
House arrest in a villa, not imprisonment. He continued writing. His Dialogues circulated widely. Rehabilitation
Pope John Paul II formally rehabilitated Galileo in 1992, calling the affair "a sad misunderstanding that now belongs to the past." The deeper point
The Galileo affair was a genuine failure. The Church has acknowledged it. It does not prove science and faith are inherently opposed.
Faith & AI

The Church enters the age of artificial intelligence

In every era of major technological change, the Catholic Church has asked the same question: does this serve human dignity, or diminish it? The printing press, the industrial revolution, nuclear weapons, bioethics - and now artificial intelligence.

Technology is never neutral, because it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, regulate, and use it. - Pope Leo XIV, Magnifica Humanitas (2026)

Pope Leo XIV signed Magnifica Humanitas on May 15, 2026, the 135th anniversary of Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum, the Church's landmark teaching on labor and industrial capitalism. It was published May 25, 2026, and is the first papal encyclical on artificial intelligence.

The encyclical is not a condemnation of AI. Leo XIV calls technology "not a force antagonistic to humanity" and affirms that it can serve genuine human flourishing. But he identifies the conditions under which AI becomes a threat: when it concentrates power in the hands of a few, displaces workers without concern for their dignity, generates misinformation at scale, or is weaponized for geopolitical domination.

Work and Dignity

AI and the future of human labor

Turn over
The concern

Rapid automation could displace workers and reshape labor markets in ways that leave many in "forced inactivity," undermining both human dignity and social stability.

The principle

Work is not merely economic. It is how human beings participate in creation and provide for family and community. Its loss has spiritual and social costs.

Leo XIV's words

"The pursuit of greater profits cannot justify choices that systematically sacrifice jobs."

Echoes Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum, 135 years later.
Power and Access

AI must not concentrate power in few hands

Turn over
The concern

A handful of companies and governments control most advanced AI. This concentration threatens both democracy and the principle that technology should serve the common good.

The principle

Catholic social teaching insists on the universal destination of goods - that property cannot be private, and that its use must serve all, not only those who hold it.

Leo XIV's words

He insists on ensuring technologies are not concentrated "in the hands of only a few people, thereby widening the gap between those included and those excluded."

The common good extends to digital goods.
Truth and Misinformation

AI's threat to truth, especially for the young

Turn over
The concern

AI can generate false text, images, audio, and video indistinguishable from real content. This poses particular dangers for children forming their understanding of reality.

The principle

Catholicism holds that truth is not a preference. Truth is a condition of human flourishing. A world saturated in synthetic falsehood is a world hostile to the human person.

Leo XIV's words

He raises concern about AI-generated misinformation and "its impact on children and young people."

There is no humanity without shared reality.
Disarm AI

Preventing technology from dominating humanity

Turn over
The concern

AI development is driven partly by geopolitical competition between superpowers and commercial competition between corporations. Both logics risk treating AI as a weapon rather than a tool.

The principle

The Church's just war tradition and its consistent opposition to weapons of mass destruction extend to any technology capable of mass harm.

Leo XIV's words

"To disarm does mean rejecting technology, and preventing it from dominating humanity."

The question is who or what AI ultimately serves.

Magnifica Humanitas continues what the Church has done in every era of major technological change: asking whether this new thing serves or diminishes the human person, and what the answer means for how AI is governed, who profits, and who gets left behind.
