Difficult Questions

If you are here because something hurt you, or because something doesn't add up, or because you've been waiting for someone to stop pretending - this chapter is for you.

Every serious person has them. The questions that don't resolve. The things that happened that no explanation touches. The gap between what the Church says it is and what it has sometimes been. This chapter will not give you pat answers, because there aren't any. What it will try to do is hold the questions honestly, without flinching and without pretending the faith is something smaller than it claims to be.

Do not be afraid of honest doubt. Be afraid of the comfortable conclusion that lets you stop asking. - Adapted from Paul Tillich

A faith that can only survive in the absence of hard questions is not a faith worth having. Catholicism has been tested by fifteen hundred years of history, philosophy, science, and its own failures. It is still here. That is not proof of anything. But it is worth noticing.

Suffering

The question no one has answered

Why does a good God allow suffering? Specifically: why does he allow the suffering of people who did nothing to deserve it? Children. The innocent. People crushed by forces entirely outside their control. This is not a clever philosophical puzzle. It is the question behind a lot of the real leaving that happens - an argument, and a wound.

Catholic theology does not have a satisfying answer to this question. Neither does anyone else. Atheism does not resolve it; it simply removes the question of why God didn't prevent it and leaves the suffering itself, unexplained and unredeemed. The question of innocent suffering runs through every worldview, not only religious ones.

What the tradition does not say
That suffering is God's punishment. That it builds character in a way that makes it worth it. That there is a reason behind every specific tragedy adequate to the tragedy. These are not Catholic positions. They are ways of escaping the weight of the question. What Job learned
The oldest sustained treatment of innocent suffering in Scripture ends not with an explanation but with an encounter. God does not answer Job's questions. He shows up. The book of Job is not a theodicy. It is the testimony of someone who went through the worst and found that they were not finally abandoned - though it did not feel that way from inside it. The Cross
Catholic faith does not offer suffering as something to be explained. It offers a God who entered it. The Crucifixion is evidence that God is not watching from a safe distance; whatever suffering is, he has been in it. Lament is biblical
"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" Those are words from the Cross, quoting the Psalms. The tradition makes room for the person who is furious at God, who feels abandoned, who cannot pray anything except that. That cry is not a failure of faith. It is what faith looks like when everything has gone wrong. The resurrection matters here
The Christian claim is not that suffering is fine, or meaningful, or educational. It is that death - the worst outcome - has been broken. If that is true, suffering still hurts exactly as much as it hurts, and it is also not the end of the story. That is the only hope the tradition offers. It is not a small one. The moral intuition beneath the question
Even the person who rejects God because of suffering is appealing to a standard - a sense that things should not be this way, that the suffering of children matters, that injustice is real. That moral intuition does not appear from nowhere. It is worth asking what it is attached to.
The Scandal

The abuse crisis, named plainly

Priests abused children. In many cases, bishops knew and moved perpetrators rather than removing them. The institution protected itself. Real children were harmed in ways they have carried their entire lives. This happened at significant scale across many countries and across decades. It was not isolated.

This site will not soften that, compare it to other institutions, or offer it as a reason to dismiss the whole faith. It states it plainly because the people it happened to deserve that, and because any version of Catholicism that cannot say it clearly is not telling the truth.

The scale
Investigations in the United States, Ireland, France, Germany, and Australia have documented thousands of cases spanning decades. The French CIASE Report (2021) estimated 330,000 victims over 70 years. The John Jay Report (US, 2004) and the Ryan Report (Ireland, 2009) are similarly exhaustive. These are documented findings, not allegations. The cover-up
In many cases the abuse itself was compounded by what followed: perpetrators transferred rather than removed, victims pressured into silence, complaints not reported to civil authorities, and an institutional logic that prioritized reputation over the safety of children. This was a moral failure at the highest institutional levels. What has happened since
Mandatory reporting requirements, review boards, removal of many perpetrators from ministry, formal apologies, and compensation programs have been implemented in most countries. Pope Francis has met personally with survivors and issued landmark child protection documents. Whether this constitutes adequate accountability is a question survivors have the primary right to answer. What has not happened
Full accountability for bishops who enabled cover-ups remains incomplete. Many survivors feel the institutional response has been more about management than genuine reckoning. The anger and grief of survivors is not irrational. This wound is not closed. The theological question
How does an institution that claims to carry the Gospel produce this? Catholic theology has always held that the Church is holy because of Christ, not because of the people in it - that grace and sin coexist in the same institution, and always have. That theological claim does not reduce the harm. It explains why the harm does not, by itself, make the Gospel false. If you were harmed
You do not owe the institution your continued presence, your forgiveness on any particular timeline, or your silence. SNAP (Survivors Network of those Abused by Priests) exists at snapnetwork.org. You are not alone, and you were not at fault.
Doubt

What to do when you're not sure

Most people who have sustained faith in God also have - or have had - periods of real doubt. vague uncertainty, and the genuine experience of looking at everything the faith claims and being unable to believe it. The tradition does not treat doubt as the opposite of faith. It treats faith as something practiced in the presence of doubt, not in its absence.

Mother Teresa's Darkness

Fifty years of doubt inside one of history's most visible lives of faith

Turn over
What she wrote

In letters not intended for publication, Teresa described decades of feeling no presence of God - "darkness," "loneliness," "torture." She called it her most painful cross.

What she did

She kept going. because the feeling returned, and because she had chosen to act as if the faith were true even when it felt false.

What it means for you

If one of the most celebrated Christians of the 20th century spent fifty years feeling nothing, your doubt does not disqualify you. It may place you in very good company.

Her doubt is documented. So is her life.
Doubt as the Beginning

Some of the most committed Catholics arrived through serious questioning

Turn over
The pattern

Chesterton, Newman, Stein, Augustine - people who became serious Catholics often passed through serious doubt first. The faith they arrived at was forged in the resistance, not in the absence of it.

The question worth asking

Is your doubt moving toward something, or staying still? Doubt that drives inquiry is different from doubt used as a reason to stop asking. Both exist. Only one of them is honest.

What the tradition asks

Keep going. Read. Pray even when it feels useless. Talk to someone. Don't settle for a comfortable exit if the questions are still alive in you.

Doubt that asks questions is different from doubt that closes doors.
The Bible Makes Room for It

Lament, confusion, and protest are part of the scriptural record

Turn over
Psalm 88

The only Psalm that ends in darkness with no resolution. The writer cries out to God and receives no answer. The last word of the Psalm is "darkness." The Church kept it in the canon.

What that means

The tradition is not interested in sanitizing human experience. It has always known that faith includes seasons of real darkness, and it preserved a literature of people who went through them.

The invitation

You are allowed to be in Psalm 88. You are not required to perform Psalm 23. Wherever you are, the tradition has been there before you and kept a record of it.

The darkest psalm ends in darkness. The Church kept it anyway.
"Help my unbelief"

The most honest prayer in the Gospels - and it was answered

Turn over
The moment

A father brings his suffering son to Jesus. Jesus asks if he believes. The man says: "I believe; help my unbelief." He does not pretend. He does not perform certainty. He says both things at once.

What Jesus does

He heals the boy. He does not first require the father to resolve his doubt. He meets him where he is.

What this means

If you can say "I want to believe; I'm not sure I do" - you have said enough to begin. That prayer has been prayed by serious people for two thousand years and found to be sufficient.

He brought both the faith and the doubt. Jesus answered anyway.

Hard Edges

Questions the tradition holds without easy resolution

Some questions in Catholic faith have careful, developed answers. Others have honest, unsatisfying ones. A few have answers that most people find troubling even after they understand them. This section does not paper over any of them.

Hell
Catholic teaching holds that hell is real and that human beings can choose it - that love cannot be forced, and that God will not drag anyone into communion against their will. The Church does not definitively say any particular person is there. What makes hell terrible in this framing is that it is chosen, not imposed. Those who never heard the Gospel
The Catechism teaches that people who, through no fault of their own, do not know Christ but seek truth and do good "may achieve eternal salvation" (CCC 847). This is not saying all religions are equally true. It is saying that God's mercy is not limited to those who received the right information. The tradition resists tidy answers in both directions here. The Church's political history
The Inquisition, the Crusades, the Church's historical alignment with oppressive regimes - these are real. The tradition acknowledges them. Most historians also note that the popular accounts are inaccurate in both directions: more nuanced than the legend of pure evil, and not as innocent as defenders claim. Women and the priesthood
The Church's position that ordination is reserved for men is experienced by many Catholics as a serious injustice. The official teaching holds that this is not a matter of dignity but of role. This is a live disagreement within the Church, not a closed question for most of its members, and it deserves to be named as such. Hypocrisy
The gap between Catholic teaching and Catholic behavior is real. Practiced Catholics often look statistically similar to everyone else across a range of moral measures. The tradition's response is that this is evidence of sin, not evidence that the teaching is wrong. It is also evidence that belonging to the Church is no guarantee of holiness - which the Church has always taught. Unanswered prayer
People pray for healing and do not receive it. They pray for reconciliation and the marriage ends. They pray for the child and the child dies. This is not a fringe experience. The tradition does not explain it away. It holds that prayer is not a mechanism for outcomes, and that God's response is real but not always legible from inside the waiting.
Hope

Why the Church is still here

After this chapter, it is worth asking why the Catholic Church still exists. Not defensively. As a genuine historical question.

Institutions that have experienced the failures the Catholic Church has experienced - at this scale, over fifteen centuries, on every continent - do not typically survive. The Church has produced saints in the same centuries it produced abusers. It has carried the works of mercy through the same institution that covered up crimes against children. It has been the home of the greatest art in Western civilization and the scene of some of its greatest betrayals.

The Church is not a gallery of saints. It is a field hospital. The question is whether it is actually healing people. - Pope Francis

The honest answer is: sometimes yes and sometimes no. There are people alive today whose lives were remade by this faith - by a priest who showed up, a community that held them, a sacrament that landed differently than expected, a long tradition of prayer that gave them a structure when everything else collapsed. There are also people whose lives were damaged by this institution, sometimes permanently. Both are true. Any account of Catholicism that cannot hold both is selling something.

The hope the tradition offers is not that the institution is reliable. The institution is made of people and people fail. The hope is older and stranger. It is the claim that a man who was executed on a Friday was encountered alive on a Sunday, and that if that is true, the worst things that happen are not the final word on anything. That claim is either the most important thing ever said or it is nothing. The tradition asks you to consider it seriously before deciding.

If you are still here after this chapter, still reading, still asking, that itself is something. The faith does not ask you to have it all resolved before you take a next step. It asks you to be honest about where you are and to keep moving toward the truth, wherever it leads. That is enough to begin.

Go Deeper

For the person who is still asking

If you were harmed by someone in the Church - by abuse, by a community that failed you, by a priest who betrayed your trust - you are not obligated to return to that community, to forgive on anyone else's timeline, or to keep your experience private. SNAP (Survivors Network of those Abused by Priests) provides support at snapnetwork.org. Many dioceses have victim assistance coordinators. Therapy with someone who understands religious trauma can help. You are not alone, and what happened to you was not your fault.

If you were hurt in ways that don't reach the threshold of abuse - by a cold community, a priest who said the wrong thing in a moment of loss, a teaching delivered without mercy - those wounds are also real. Sometimes the honest next step is finding a different parish, a spiritual director, or simply some time away.

This is the right question to end with, because everything in Christianity stands or falls on it. Paul says so explicitly: "If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Corinthians 15:17).

The historical case is more serious than most people assume. The tomb was empty - not disputed even by hostile ancient sources. The disciples died for their claim that they had encountered Jesus alive. People die for things they believe; it is rare to die for something you know to be fabricated. The early Church's claim was specific, public, and made in the same city where the crucifixion happened, within the lifetimes of people who could have contradicted it.

None of that proves the resurrection. Historical evidence doesn't work that way. But it deserves serious engagement, not dismissal. N.T. Wright's The Resurrection of the Son of God is the most rigorous modern historical treatment. It is long. It is worth it.

Then you are in an honest place. That is not nothing.

The tradition would ask: have you genuinely tried prayer - not as a technique but as an address to someone you suspect might be there? Have you found a community of people whose lives show evidence of what faith claims to produce? Have you read the serious thinkers - Augustine, Newman, Chesterton, Edith Stein - and let the arguments actually land?

If you have done those things and remain unconvinced, the Church asks you to keep living as honestly and as well as you can, and to stay open. Truths have a way of finding people who are genuinely looking for them, even when the search takes longer than expected.
